01

Product brief

THE PRODUCT IN ONE MINUTE

Professional access without the bottomless feed.

A voluntary LinkedIn session with two visible bounds: elapsed time and posts viewed. The product succeeds when people finish intentionally and remember what mattered.

People often need LinkedIn for job search, professional identity, and weak-tie maintenance. The same feed that provides that access is designed without a natural stopping point. For someone already managing attention regulation, the cost is not simply screen time. It is losing the original task.

USER NEED

Reach professional value without surrendering the session.

PLATFORM CONSTRAINT

Preserve familiar posts, reactions, and creator signals.

SUCCESS

Recall, comprehension, intentional completion, settledness.

NON-GOAL

Diagnose attention, moralize use, or optimize engagement.

Design question

Can LinkedIn keep its professional utility while making the end of a visit visible from the start?

03

System anatomy

FRAME / MODULE / JOINT / TRACE

The finish line is product infrastructure.

The product has four modules: Bound the session, Focus the feed, Return chosen posts, and Close without scolding.

  1. 01 / BOUND

    Choose time and post limits.

    Five, 12, or 20 minutes map to eight, 15, or 25 posts. The first limit reached closes the session.

  2. 02 / FOCUS

    Make one post the active module.

    Progress, time, and the saved count stay visible. Secondary actions appear only after intent.

  3. 03 / RETURN

    Let reactions seed future attention.

    Insightful, Support, and Love place the post into a private fixed-interval queue. Like, Celebrate, and Funny remain ephemeral.

  4. 04 / CLOSE

    End cleanly at either boundary.

    A midpoint check-in appears once. Closure summarizes what happened and lets the person leave, review saved posts, or choose a new session.

TRACEAuthored prototype defaults
NOWD+3D+7D+14

The literature supports spacing as a general memory strategy. It does not validate this exact schedule for social posts or ADHD. The intervals are design defaults to test.

04

Key decisions

LOAD-BEARING CHOICES

Three decisions do most of the work.

Three decisions carry the concept: dual session bounds, an on-demand Action Dock, and reactions that make future attention explicit.

01

Two bounds, one actual stop.

Choice: expose time and post count together, then close at whichever arrives first.

Why: a timer alone can become another demand. A post bound makes the amount of content legible; a time bound prevents slow content from turning the session infinite.

Tradeoff: some sessions end before a person reaches a desired post. Starting another bounded session stays explicit.

02

One dock, closed by default.

Choice: collapse six reaction choices behind one 56px Action Dock trigger.

Why: reacting is the rare path. Six standing targets asked for a decision on every post before the person had read it.

Tradeoff: reactions take one more action. The common reading and move-on paths get a much quieter resting state.

03

Turn meaning into future attention.

Choice: reuse three semantically rich reactions as an explicit save-for-return action.

Why: the signal already exists at the moment something matters. The user sees the consequence before committing and can remove queued items.

Tradeoff: one reaction now has two meanings. Production copy, consent, retention, and queue controls are mandatory.

End-to-end screens

Entry, setup, active session, midpoint, and closure screens. These are production-fidelity concepts, not integrated LinkedIn UI.

SYNTHETIC

Measurement rehearsal

A fixed-seed event fixture exercises validation, transforms, and charts. Its apparent effects are authored and are not research results.

OPEN

Human outcomes

No representative-user study yet. Recall, comprehension, settledness, and perceived control remain hypotheses.

OPEN

Platform viability

No LinkedIn integration, privacy review, economic model, or production telemetry.

The coded artifact supports a design argument. It does not convert literature, synthetic fixtures, or implementation quality into evidence about people.

06

Option space

KILLED WITH REASONS

The quietest direction survived.

The permanent reaction tray, passive timer, and engagement-first success model were rejected because each weakened the core capability thesis.

KILLED / A

Permanent reaction tray

Fast to discover, costly at rest. It placed six choices on every card, so the rare action dominated the common reading path.

KILLED / B

Timer-only wellbeing mode

Easy to ship, too easy to ignore or experience as a scolding overlay. It changed the chrome, not the feed's stopping structure.

KILLED / C

Time-on-app as the primary KPI

Simple for a dashboard, contradictory for the product. Engagement remains a business guardrail, never the success definition.

SELECTED

Bounded session + Action Dock

More system work, but one coherent model: visible limits, a quiet resting state, meaningful return, and explicit closure.

08

Evaluation

MEASUREMENT PLAN, NOT RESULTS

Test the riskiest assumption before the growth story.

A future study should test capability first, treat engagement only as a guardrail, and define harm thresholds before enrollment.

PRIMARY

Did the session help?

  • Resurfaced-content recall
  • Comprehension after reflow
  • Intentional completion
  • Settledness change
GUARDRAIL

Did platform value collapse?

  • Opt-in and completion
  • Reaction-mix change
  • Resurface acceptance
  • Task completion
HARM SIGNAL

Did the design make things worse?

  • Compulsive re-entry
  • Dismissal after resurfacing
  • Worsened settledness
  • Loss of perceived control

Proposed first study

Run a counterbalanced task study with representative participants before testing retention. Compare bounded and standard-feed conditions on equivalent professional tasks. Set exclusion rules, primary outcomes, analysis, harm thresholds, and stop conditions before enrollment. Follow with interviews about control, trust, reaction meaning, and closure.

09

Responsibility

PRIVATE ATTENTION NEEDS PRIVATE RULES

A private attention system has to behave like one.

A production version requires explicit consent, legible queue consequences, deletion and pause controls, data minimization, and accessible alternatives.

CONSENT

Tell people what a reaction will do.

The return consequence appears before commit. Participation is off by default and reversible.

CONTROL

Make the queue visible, editable, pausable, and deletable.

These controls are requirements for production, not features already implemented in the interaction study.

DATA

Store the minimum and define retention.

Do not train models on post text, reactions, attention patterns, or self-declared health context without separate informed consent.

ACCESS

Every gesture needs an equivalent control.

Tap and keyboard routes, visible focus, screen-reader names, reduced motion, and generous targets are part of the interaction contract.

AI boundary

Summarization or probe grading could be explored only after privacy review. Session bounds, reaction meaning, scheduling, queue ownership, and closure remain deterministic and user-controlled.
